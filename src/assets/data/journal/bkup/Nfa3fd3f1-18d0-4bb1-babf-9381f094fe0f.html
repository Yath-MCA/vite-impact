Smoking (nicotine) has been reported to possibly be neuroprotective and conducive to patients with early Parkinson’s disease (PD). However, the causal effect of smoking on PD and the molecular mechanisms of smoking-related genes (SRGs) are vague.
First, genome-wide association study summary data on smoking (ukb-b-6244) and PD (ieu-b-7) were retrieved from the Integrative Epidemiology Unit OpenGWAS database for Mendelian randomization (MR) analysis. Sensitivity analyses were performed to validate the results of the MR analyses. Subsequently, a differential analysis of PD patients and controls was performed to identify differentially expressed SRGs (DE-SRGs). Finally, the expression of DE-SRGs was analyzed in annotated cell types.
The MR analysis revealed that smoking was a protective factor causally related to PD (p = .008, odds ratio = 0.288). Furthermore, a total of five DE-SRGs enriched in Toll-like receptor signaling pathways were identified in GSE7621 dataset. Regarding the single-cell analysis of the GSE184950 dataset, a total of nine cell types were annotated. The expression of LRRN1 in oligodendrocyte progenitor cells and oligodendrocytes, respectively, differed significantly between PD patients and controls.
Our study supported a causal relationship between smoking and PD and found that five SRGs (MAPK8IP1, LRRN1, LINC00324, HIST1H2BK, and YOD1) enriched in Toll-like receptor signaling pathways might be beneficial in PD. In addition, single-cell sequencing indicated that four SRGs were differentially expressed in different cell types. All four genes except MAPK8IP1 were significantly correlated with the 10 genes calculated by scPagwas. Thus, this evidence provides a theoretical basis for further research on the effect of nicotine (smoking) on PD.
In order to explore the potential etiology and pathogenesis of Parkinson’s disease, this study combined MR analysis, transcriptomics, and single-cell sequencing analysis to explore the association between exposure factors and Parkinson’s disease, observe and confirm the relationship and mechanism between the two from the perspective of genetics, and provide more reliable evidence for causal inference.

Key Points
  • Two-sample Mendelian randomization showed that smoking was causally associated with a depressed risk of Parkinson’s disease (PD).
  • Five differentially expressed smoking-related genes were identified, mainly enriched in Toll-like receptor signaling pathways.
  • Single-cell RNA-Seq data suggested a potential mechanism involving oligodendrocyte progenitor cells and oligodendrocytes in PD.
  • The scPagwas analysis of scRNA-seq data identifies cell types and genes significantly associated with PD.
Introduction
Neurodegenerative diseases are now the leading source of disability globally, with Parkinson’s disease (PD) being the fastest-growing neurological disorder in the world. From 1990 to 2015, the number of people with PD doubled to over 6 million. As population aging accelerates, this number is estimated to double again to over 12 million by 2040.1 PD is a common neurodegenerative disease characterized by the degeneration of dopaminergic neurons in the midbrain, with pathological features of abnormal α-synuclein deposition and Lewy body lesions. The clinical features of PD include motor symptoms such as static tremor, slow movement, muscle rigidity, and postural gait abnormalities, as well as non-motor symptoms such as cognitive dysfunction, depression, and sleep disorders.2 However, the pathophysiology and risk factors of PD are still elusive. Recent epidemiological studies have shown that smokers have a lower incidence of neurodegenerative diseases than nonsmokers, as well as a similar association between PD and passive smoking.3 Moreover, the higher the daily level of smoking, the lower the risk of PD,4 and this finding is more obvious in fraternal twins. These studies all emphasize the existence of an association between smoking and PD development, but the specific mechanism is not clear and the causal relationship between the two needs to be further clarified. Mendelian randomization (MR) is considered to be a promising epidemiological method for studying exposure factors and disease. It is a causal inference method based on genetic variation, wherein the influence of biological factors on disease can be inferred using the influence of randomly assigned genotypes on phenotypes in nature.5
Nicotine is one of the main components of tobacco and can be used as a drug to relieve PD due to its good blood–brain barrier transmission and short half-life. Previous observational and epidemiological studies have found associations between smoking and PD[6, 7, 8, 9]. The reasons for tobacco use having a potential protective effect against PD may be related to several pathophysiological mechanisms. First, nicotine activates the nicotinic acetylcholine receptor, which is associated with the release of a variety of neurotransmitters, including acetylcholine, dopamine, norepinephrine, gamma-aminobutyric acid, glutamine, and serotonin, and can alter synaptic plasticity.10 Second, nicotine can inhibit inflammation induced by various exogenous stimuli.11
Thus, to further explore the potential etiology and pathogenesis of PD, our present study combined MR analysis, transcriptomics, and single-cell sequencing data to explore the relationship between smoking and PD. This study may help clinicians in PD prevention and pharmacologists in PD drug development.
Materials and Methods
Data Sources
The Gene Expression Omnibus database (https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/) was employed to retrieve the expression profiles of the substantia nigra of 16 PD and nine normal samples in GSE7621 dataset,12 as well as the single-cell transcriptomics of the substantia nigra of 24 PD and 10 normal samples in GSE184950 dataset. The genome-wide association study (GWAS) summary data on PD and smoking were obtained from the Integrative Epidemiology Unit OpenGWAS (https://gwas.mrcieu.ac.uk/). The data on PD (ieu-b-7) comprised 33 674 cases, 449 056 controls, and 17 891 936 single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs). The data on smoking (ukb-b-6244) encompassed 391 502 samples and 9 851 867 SNPs.
GWAS Data Preprocessing and MR Analysis
The “extract_instruments” function in the “TwoSampleMR” R package was used to assess the data on smoking (ukb-b-6244) and screen SNPs markedly associated with smoking (p < 5 × 10–6) as instrumental variables (IVs),13 followed by the elimination of IVs with linkage disequilibrium (clump = TRUE; r2 = 0.001; kb = 10 000). Then, the corresponding SNPs of PD were obtained and combined with the abovementioned SNPs related to smoking, abiding by the principles of whether the alleles of exposure and outcome were the same and whether the SNPs had palindromic structures. Thereafter, the “harmonise_data” function in “TwoSampleMR” was utilized to harmonize the effect equipotential with effect size. Five algorithms combined with the “mr” function were used for MR analysis, including MR Egger,14 weighted median,15 inverse variance weighted (IVW),16 simple mode,13 and maximum likelihood.17 Of these, the IVW method was decisive for causal inference regarding smoking and PD. The results were presented using scatter plots, forest plots, and funnel plots. Finally, a sensitivity analysis was carried out to illustrate the reliability of the above MR results, including heterogeneity, horizontal pleiotropy, and leave-one-out (LOO) analyses.
Acquisition and Enrichment Analysis of SRGs
The underlying molecular mechanism was explored to further evaluate the effect of smoking on PD. First, the eQTL mapping method was used to annotate the nicotine-related SNPs into gene symbols accompanied by significant cis-eQTLs in the eQTLGen Consortium database. These genes in GSE7621 dataset, that is, the SRGs, were included in the subsequent differential analysis. The differentially expressed SRGs (DE-SRGs) between PD and normal samples in GSE7621 dataset were identified using the “limma” R package18 (|log2(fold change)| > 0.5, p < .01), followed by the creation of a volcano plot and heat map. In order to expound the molecular function and signaling pathway of SRGs, the enrichment analysis was performed based on Gene Ontology (GO) and the Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG) databases through “ClusterProfiler.”19 Simultaneously, the single-gene gene set enrichment analysis (GSEA) for DE-SRGs was carried out using “ClusterProfiler” based on the correlation between the DE-SRGs and all genes in the GSE7621 dataset, with reference to the KEGG gene set from the MSigDB database. This step was performed to excavate regulatory pathways or biological functions associated with the expression of DE-SRGs (p < .05). In addition, the correlation between DE-SRGs in the GSE7621 dataset was assessed with the help of Spearman’s method (|cor| > 0.3, p < .05).
Expression of DE-SRGs in Different Cells and Functions of Cells in GSE184950 Dataset Based on Single-Cell RNA-seq
The expression of DE-SRGs in the different cells in the GSE184950 dataset was assessed. First, the following quality control criteria were applied to the single-cell RNA-seq dataset GSE184950: (1) cells in which the unique molecular identifier (UMI) count was lower than 200 and greater than 50 000 were filtered out; (2) cells in which the number of expressed genes was greater than 200 and less than 5000 were screened out; and (3) cells in which the proportion of mitochondrial genes was less than 10% were reserved. The “FindVariableFeatures” function and “vst” method were employed to identify the top 2000 highly variable genes among the cells, and the results were presented as scatter plots. To better evaluate and analyze cell groups, linear dimensionality reduction was performed based on gene expression in each cell using the “JackStraw” and “JackStrawPlot” functions, followed by cell clustering analysis based on the cell expression matrix after dimensionality reduction with the “FindNeighbors” and “FindClusters” functions (resolution = 1).20 Afterward, the cell types were annotated with the help of the marker genes in a previous study.21 The proportions of different cell types and the expression levels of the DE-SRGs in annotated cells in the PD and control groups were compared. Furthermore, gene set variation analysis (GSVA)22 was performed to explore the biological functions of cell clusters in the GSE184950 dataset based on “hallmark gene sets” (p < .05), following which cell pseudotime trajectory and cell communication analyses were put into effect using “SCORPIUS and “CellChat,”23 respectively. Our research ideas and general methods can be seen in the flow diagram (Supplementary Figure 1).
The scPagwas Analysis of scRNA-seq Data and GWAS Data
Pathway activity scores obtained from scRNA-seq data were subjected to a multigene linear regression with GWAS genetic signals, for identifying a set of trait-associated genes that could be used to infer trait-associated cell subpopulations. Based on the different cell types annotated in scRNA-seq, trait-related scores (TRS) were calculated for each cell type by scPagwas to assess the role of cell types in relation to PD. Further, the significance between different cell types and PD was calculated by the scPagwas_main function (p < .05). In addition, the correlation coefficients between each gene in the GSE184950 dataset and the PD were calculated using Pearson to identify the genes most associated with the PD. Furthermore, their correlation with the DE-SRGs acquired above was assessed (p < .05).
Results
Smoking Was Causally Associated With a Depressed Risk of PD
After the screening stage, a total of 67 independent SNPs related to smoking were identified as IVs for MR analysis of the causative influence of smoking on PD (Supplementary Table 1). A causal relationship between smoking and PD was observed by the IVW method (p = .008), and smoking was a protective factor for PD (odds ratio = 0.288, 95% CI = 0.115 to 0.721) (Table 1). The scatter plot of the causal effect of smoking on PD seemed to show a negative tendency in accordance with the previous MR results (Figure 1A). The forest plot revealed that the overall effect of the IVW model was prominent (Figure 1B). The valid IVs estimated by the IVW method were distributed symmetrically in the funnel plot, suggesting that the MR analysis was consistent with Mendel’s second law random grouping (Figure 1C).

The Reliability of the MR Results Was Illustrated by Sensitivity Analysis
To determine the reliability of the above MR results, a sensitivity analysis was carried out. Significant heterogeneity in the IVW model was detected among SNPs associated with smoking through Cochran’s Q test (Q = 87.454, p = .040); thus, the multiplicative random-effect IVW approach was adopted to infer its effect on PD (Supplementary Table 2). Moreover, no horizontal pleiotropy was observed in the MR Egger test (intercept = 0.006; p = .643), meaning no confounding factors existed (Supplementary Table 3). Upon eliminating single SNPs step by step by the LOO method, no exaggerated influence on the model effect was observed (Supplementary Figure 2). In conclusion, smoking was a protective factor for PD with proven reliability.
Five DE-SRGs Were Mainly Enriched in Toll-Like Receptor Signaling Pathways
The cisgene of 67 IVs was tested against the eQTLGen database and found to match 155 genes, which corresponded to a total of 36 smoking-related SNPs, of which the 117 genes in the GSE7621 dataset were incorporated into the differential expression analysis and enrichment analysis (Supplementary Table 4). Five DE-SRGs were differentially expressed between the control and disease groups, including MAPK8IP1 (upregulated), LRRN1, LINC00324, HIST1H2BK, and YOD1 (all downregulated; Supplementary Figure 3A). The expression pattern of these five DE-SRGs is shown in a heat map (Supplementary Figure 3B). Additionally, the 117 SRGs were enriched in 495 GO items (such as negative regulation of the humoral immune response, the T-cell receptor signaling pathway, and negative regulation of B-cell-mediated immunity) and 126 KEGG pathways (such as meiotic recombination, RNA polymerase I promoter escape, and Human Cytomegalovirus (HCMV) early events; Supplementary Figure 3C and D, Supplementary Tables 5 and 6). With respect to the single-gene GSEA of the five DE-SRGs, LRRN1 was enriched in 33 KEGG pathways, including ribosome, long-term potentiation, and fatty acid metabolism; MAPK8IP1 was involved in 13 pathways, such as leishmania infection, ribosome, and Toll-like receptor signaling pathways; YOD1 acted on 31 pathways, for instance, the p53 signaling pathway, spliceosome, and Toll-like receptor signaling pathways; and HIST1H2BK and LINC00324 played a role in 47 and 48 pathways, respectively, including PD, Alzheimer’s disease, and oxidative phosphorylation (Figure 2A, Supplementary Table 7). Moreover, YOD1 was most strongly positively correlated with LINC00324 (cor = 0.614, p < .01), and the strongest negative correlation was observed between MAPK8IP1 and LRRN1 (cor = -0.51, p < .01; Figure 2B).

Four DE-SRGs Were Differentially Expressed in Different Cell Types in GSE184950 Dataset Except for HIST1H2BK
After quality control, a total of 23 315 genes and 24 144 cells in the GSE184950 dataset were obtained for follow-up analysis (Figure 3A), and the top 10 highly variable genes were labeled in a scatter diagram (Figure 3B). After dimensionality reduction analysis, all cells in the GSE184950 dataset were classified into 38 clusters (Figure 3C, Supplementary Table 8). The resulting cell clusters were annotated into nine cell types, including T cells, oligodendrocytes, endothelial cells, astrocytes, microglia, oligodendrocyte progenitor cells, neurons, pericytes, and fibroblast-like cells (Figure 3D), in which the expression level of marker genes was suitable (Figure 3E, Supplementary Table 9). Supplementary Table 10 illustrates the number of cells contained in the different cell types. Subsequently, the proportion of oligodendrocytes was found to be dominant in the PD and control groups (Figure 3F), while fibroblast-like cells differed between the two groups (Figure 3G). Furthermore, the expression of the five DE-SRGs was evaluated in the different cell types for both groups. From the transcriptome, MAPK8IP1 was found to be down-regulated in the disease, whereas the other four genes were up-regulated in the disease. At the single-cell level, MAPK8IP1 was downregulated in astrocytes, oligodendrocyte progenitor cells, and oligodendrocytes. lRRN1 was upregulated in astrocytes, neurons, oligodendrocyte progenitor cells, oligodendrocytes, and T cells. LINC00324 was upregulated in astrocytes, endothelial cells, neurons, oligodendrocyte progenitor cells, oligodendrocytes, and pericytes. YOD1 was up-regulated in astrocytes, endothelial cells, oligodendrocyte progenitor cells, oligodendrocytes, and pericytes (Supplementary Figures 4A, B and 5).

Potential Role of Oligodendrocyte Progenitor Cells and Oligodendrocytes in PD
Oligodendrocytes were enriched in 48 entries (43 with upregulation and five with downregulation), including androgen response, early estrogen response, and Mechanistic Target of Rapamycin Complex 1 (mTORC1) signaling; oligodendrocyte progenitor cells were enriched in 37 entries (35 with upregulation and two with downregulation), such as hypoxia, mTORC1 signaling, and TNFA signaling via NFKB (Supplementary Figure 7A). Incidentally, the function of the seven other cell types was also explored via GSVA (Supplementary Figure 6). Regarding the pseudotime trajectory analysis, oligodendrocyte progenitor cells were found to transform into oligodendrocytes (Supplementary Figure 7B), and LRRN1, MAPK8IP1, and LINC00324 were highly expressed in oligodendrocyte progenitor cells, oligodendrocytes, and differentiation state, respectively (Supplementary Figure 7C). Further, oligodendrocyte progenitor cells and oligodendrocytes interacted with each other, as well as with endothelial cells, astrocytes, pericytes, and T cells in the PD and control groups (Supplementary Figure 7D).
Identification of Cell Types and Genes Significantly Associated With PD by scPagwas
We calculated the TRS for each cell type and found that oligodendrocyte progenitor cells, astrocytes, fibroblast-like cells, and neurons had a relatively high TRS, suggesting some correlative association with PD (Supplementary Figure 8A and B). Further, astrocytes, neurons and oligodendrocyte progenitor cells were proved to be remarkably associated with the PD by the calculation of scPagwas_main function (p < .05) (Supplementary Figure 8C). Next, the correlation of all the genes in the GSE184950 dataset with the disease was calculated, among which CTNNA2, PLCB1, KCND2, KCNIP4, FGF14, FAM155A, NRXN1, CA10, OPCML, and NRCAM were most correlated with the disease (Supplementary Figure 8D). Moreover, LRRN1, LINC00324, HIST1H2BK, and YOD1 were significantly associated with these genes, except for MAPK8IP1 (p < .05) (Supplementary Figure 8E).
Discussion
PD is a neurodegenerative disease that poses a serious threat to human health, causing great physical and mental suffering in patients and placing a heavy financial burden on families. Nicotine, the main component of cigarettes, plays a neuroprotective role against PD. The correlation between the two parameters and the underlying mechanism is still unclear. We used MR in this study to reveal the mechanism of smoking’s influence on PD and provide a theoretical basis for clinical treatment and drug development. Transcriptome data indicated that five DE-SRGs were mainly enriched in Toll-like receptor signaling pathways. To further explore which cell subsets are affected by smoking, we analyzed the expression of DE-SRGs in different cells and their cellular functions and found that oligodendrocyte progenitor cells and oligodendrocytes may play important roles in PD.
A recent article evaluated the influence of changes in smoking and alcohol consumption on the risk of PD.24 We observed a similar protective effect of smoking against PD utilizing a stringent instrument selection, large GWAS sample and various sensitivity analyses, more MR methods, and no horizontal pleiotropy. In addition, although heterogeneity emerged during our study, this may be due to the fact that populations may have a population structure, ie, there are differences in gene frequencies between different subgroups, which may affect the relationship between genotype and exposure. However, because we chose to use the random effect of IVW here, which can identify the SNPs that produce heterogeneity and weight them less in the causal judgement to circumvent the risk posed by heterogeneity to ensure reliable and stable results. There is also evidence of a protective effect of smoking on the risk of PD.
After obtaining the causal relationship between exposure and outcome using MR analysis, we further explored the protective effect of smoking on PD at the genetic level. Five DE-SRGs (MAPK8IP1, LRRN1, LINC00324, HIST1H2BK, and YOD1) were mainly enriched in Toll-like receptor signaling pathways. Moreover, four DE-SRGs were significantly associated with PD by scPagwas (LRRN1, LINC00324, HIST1H2BK, and YOD1), except for MAPK8IP1. Studies have shown that LRRN1 is downregulated in neuroepithelial cells in the boundary regions of the ventricle layer, which is associated with delayed neurodevelopment or neuroinhibition.25 Nevertheless, more evidence is required to confirm whether LRRN1 is involved in the pathophysiology of PD. The function of LINC00324 has not been reported in the context of neurological diseases, but abnormal LINC00324 expression is significantly associated with cancer risk in most cancers. LINC00324 can participate in the regulation of four signaling pathways in cancer, including the PI3K/AKT signaling pathway, p53 signaling pathway, cell cycle regulatory pathway, and Notch signaling pathway. These pathways and pathogenic events are related to the pathogenesis of PD.26 HIST1H2BK has been reported in neurodegenerative disease, wherein postmortem brain extracts from neurodegenerative disease patients were inoculated into mouse brains to produce a prion-like Aβ diffusion effect.27 As a group of epigenetic targets related to transcription regulators, histone deacetylases play an important role in chromatin remodeling and gene expression by regulating histone acetylation status, which may be involved in the pathogenesis of PD. In addition, histone deacetylase inhibitors are being developed for the treatment of PD, such as sodium butyrate, valproic acid, and phenylbutyrate.28–31 We can infer that HIST1H2BK may also be similar to the chemical modification of histones and participate in the occurrence of PD. Studies have shown the presence of anti-YOD1 antibodies in immunostained Lewy bodies in PD patients, indicating that YOD1 may participate in the pathogenesis of PD through its deubiquitinating enzyme activity.32 Further, YOD1 disrupts the stability of α-synuclein by upregulating NEDD4.33 In summary, this study has the advantage of describing the protective effects of smoking at the genetic level.
In our study, the five DE-SRGs were mainly enriched in Toll-like receptor signaling pathways. In PD, Toll-like receptors can be activated by pathogen-associated molecular patterns or injury-associated molecular patterns (such as α-synuclein) and subsequently activate microglia to produce a neuroinflammatory response. Therefore, blocking Toll-like receptors may be a specific therapy to reduce the neurotoxicity of pathologic α-synuclein in PD.34
Further, we found that among the five DE-SRGs, LRRN1 and MAPK8IP1 were highly differential expressed in oligodendrocyte progenitors and oligodendrocytes. Moreover, oligodendrocyte progenitor cells were proved to be remarkably associated with PD by the calculation of scPagwas. These results suggest that smoking may regulate the biological function and differentiation state of oligodendrocyte progenitors and oligodendrocytes. Oligodendrocyte progenitor dysfunction, resulting in inadequate differentiation of oligodendrocyte progenitors, mediates the progression of neurodegenerative diseases, such as PD, AD, and multiple sclerosis. Our study shows that DE-SRGs are highly differential expressed in oligodendrocyte progenitor cells and oligodendrocytes, and this differentiation from oligodendrocyte progenitor cells to oligodendrocytes is highly expressed in PD and control groups, suggesting that these two types of cells play a role in the genesis and development of PD. The status or degree of myelination is considered to be a key factor in determining the susceptibility of neurons in PD to Lewy body pathology.35 Further, articles have reported a significant association of oligodendrocytes and oligodendrocyte progenitors with PD-associated risk sites through single-nucleus RNA-sequencing, as well as several dysregulated genes and pathways, including regulation of tau kinase activity, regulation of viral inclusion body assembly, and processes that target mitochondrial proteins.36 All these studies have confirmed that the differentiation of oligodendrocytes and oligodendrocyte progenitors plays a regulatory role in PD, but the specific mechanism has not been fully elucidated, and our study makes up for this gap. Therefore, linked to the biological functions of genes, we speculate that the protective effect of smoking at the cellular level may be realized through the regulation of LRRN1 neuroepithelial cell signaling in oligodendrocyte progenitors, thus affecting the occurrence of PD.
Although our study produced some interesting results regarding the association between smoking and PD, its limitations should not be ignored. First, it was not possible to perform a subgroup analysis to determine the effect of smoking on PD risk considering gender and age group. Another shortcoming is that this study did not consider the impact of smoking behavior on survival; smoking is well known to lead to other serious diseases, heavy smoking increases mortality,37 and heavy smokers are underrepresented in PD. Thus, its findings should be verified and extended in future work.
Conclusions
We demonstrated a potential causal relationship between smoking and PD, with smoking being a protective factor. SRGs enriched in Toll-like receptor signaling pathways were associated with the development of PD. In addition, single-cell sequencing analysis indicated that oligodendrocyte progenitors and oligodendrocytes were also associated with PD. This evidence provides a reference for future studies of the genetic background and pathological mechanisms of PD. In future research, we look forward to extracting the main components of tobacco, and it may be used clinically to improve the symptoms of PD patients. In basic research, the pathways screened in this study can be up-regulated or down-regulated to verify the impact on PD. At the genetic level, disease-causing genes can be knocked out to observe whether the pathological process of PD has changed, which are the directions of future research and may prevent and treat PD.
