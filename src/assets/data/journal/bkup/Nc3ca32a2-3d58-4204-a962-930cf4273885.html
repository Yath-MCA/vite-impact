Clostridioides difficile infection (CDI) remains a major healthcare-associated infection, leading to severe complications. Early identification of patients at high risk for unfavorable outcomes remains challenging. Pancreatic stone protein (PSP) has emerged as a promising biomarker in sepsis; however, its role in CDI has not been investigated.
We conducted a prospective multicenter study of adult patients with CDI from 13 hospitals in Greece. PSP was measured within 24 hours of CDI onset. The primary outcome was the association between PSP and unfavorable outcome, defined as at least one of the following: organ failure, severe disease progression, relapse, or death. Receiver operating characteristic curve was performed to assess diagnostic performance, and multivariate logistic regression to evaluate independent predictors of adverse outcome.
One hundred forty-seven patients were enrolled, and unfavorable outcomes occurred in 52. PSP levels were significantly higher in these patients. PSP showed an AUC of 0.648 [95% confidence interval (CI) 0.550–0.746, P = 0.003] for predicting adverse outcome and an optimal cutoff of 300 ng/mL, which increased the risk significantly (odds ratio 3.73, 95% CI 1.822–7.638, P < 0.001). C-reactive protein (CRP) was also associated with adverse outcome (AUC 0.619, 95% CI 0.512–0.726, P: 0.03), with an optimal cutoff ≥90 mg/L. Combined elevation of PSP and CRP conferred stronger prognostic value (odds ratio 7.04, 95% CI 2.78–17.85, P < 0.001). In multivariate analysis, PSP ≥300 ng/mL (P: 0.006) and CRP ≥90 mg/L (P: 0.04) remained independently associated with unfavorable outcome.
This is the first study evaluating PSP in CDI. PSP, especially combined with CRP, may serve as complementary biomarkers for early risk stratification.

INTRODUCTION
Clostridioides difficile infection (CDI) remains one of the most common healthcare-associated infections, with significant morbidity, mortality, and risk of recurrence (1). While most cases present as mild or moderate diarrhea, a significant proportion of patients develop severe or fulminant disease, leading to complications such as toxic megacolon, intestinal perforation, sepsis, and death (2). The ability to promptly identify patients at risk for adverse outcomes remains a crucial unmet need in clinical practice.
Several scoring systems and laboratory markers have been proposed to predict the possibility of acquiring CDI or the risk of relapse (3–5). Commonly used markers such as a leukocyte count, C-reactive protein (CRP), procalcitonin, and serum creatinine levels have been evaluated for the prediction of disease severity or relapse, but not for developing organ dysfunction or death (4,6,7). Moreover, many of these markers fail to reliably predict early clinical deterioration, limiting their usefulness in guiding timely interventions.
Pancreatic stone protein (PSP), also known as Regenerating Protein Ia, is a secretory protein primarily produced by pancreatic acinar cells, although it can also be expressed in other tissues under stress conditions (8). PSP has recently emerged as a promising biomarker of infection and sepsis, demonstrating early elevation in critically ill patients and in those undergoing major surgery (9,10). Its role in modulating immune responses and in the detection of inflammation has been highlighted in several studies, showing superior diagnostic and prognostic performance compared with conventional biomarkers in selected settings (11–13).
Since the gastrointestinal tract is a major reservoir for the production of PSP (14,15), we hypothesized that PSP might serve as an early and sensitive marker for identifying patients at increased risk of poor outcomes in CDI.
PATIENTS AND METHODS
PSP was measured in a prospective collection of samples from 13 Departments of Internal Medicine during the period September 2015 to December 2016 for the SPECIFY study and during April 2022 to June 2023 for the BEYOND study. The SPECIFY study protocol was approved by the Ethics Committees of the participating hospitals (Clinicaltrials.gov NCT02573571). The BEYOND trial was approved by the National Ethics Committee (approval 195/21) of Greece and by the National Organization for Medicines (approval IS192-21) (EudraCT number 2021-005473-10, Clinicaltrials.gov NCT05304715). In both studies, patients were enrolled after written informed consent was provided by themselves or their legal representatives.
The two studies had common inclusion criteria, namely: 1) age ≥18 years old, 2) patients of both sexes, 3) at least three episodes of unformed stools in the last 24 hours according to the Bristol stool scale score (16), and 4) presence of C. difficile in stools. The latter was defined as the parallel detection of glutamate dehydrogenase and toxin A and/or B in the stools by the C. DIFF QUIK CHEK COMPLETE (Alere, Cologne, Germany) test (17). Patients were enrolled either during hospitalization or upon presentation to the hospital with CDI symptoms. None of the study participants was subject to prior surgery; patients with acute organ dysfunction were excluded.
Within 24 hours of onset of diarrhea in the patients, 5 mL of whole blood was drawn after venipuncture of one forearm vein under aseptic conditions and collected into a tube containing ethylenediaminetetraacetic acid. The blood was centrifuged at 1,300 rpm for 10 minutes to separate the plasma, which was then stored at −80°C until further analysis. PSP levels were determined from the K2-ethylenediaminetetraacetic acid anticoagulated venous plasma samples using the in vitro diagnostic IVD CAPSULE PSP capsule, which is a single-use, rapid immunofluorescence assay used together with the near-patient abioSCOPE reading platform (Abionic SA, Epalinges, Switzerland).
Each patient received treatment at the discretion of the attending physicians and he was followed up for 40 days, where clinical and laboratory data were recorded. Based on the recorded data, the Charlson Comorbidity Index (CCI) was calculated (18). The following information was recorded daily until day 40: 1) any organ failure as defined by international criteria (19), 2) treatment for CDI, 3) need for colectomy or transfer to intensive care unit, 4) clinical response or relapse, and 5) final outcome (survival/death). In case of discharge before day 40, information on the final outcome was obtained by phone calls.
The primary endpoint was to identify one plasma PSP level, which can predict the unfavorable outcome of CDI. Unfavorable outcome was defined as the incidence of any progression into organ dysfunction, CDI relapse, and/or death by day 40.
Expression of qualitative data was performed with percentages and 95% confidence limits. For quantitative data, normally distributed data were presented as means and standard deviation and non-normally distributed data as median and interquartile ranges (IQR). Comparison of the quantitative data was made with the Fisher’s exact test or Pearson chi-square test, and the qualitative data with the Student’s t test and Mann–Whitney tests depending on the normal or non-normal distribution of the values.
The performance of PSP and other biomarkers to select patients with unfavorable outcome was tested by plotting one receiver operating characteristic curve. An optimal cutoff was calculated using Youden’s Index. Sensitivity, specificity, positive predictive value, negative predictive value, and odds ratio (OR) were calculated by one 2 × 2 contingency table. To investigate the additional value of PSP to current clinical practice, a multivariate binary logistic model was performed to identify independent predictors of unfavorable outcomes. Variables that showed a statistically significant difference between patients with and without unfavorable outcomes in univariate analyses were included in the model. All statistical analyses were performed in SPSS version 31.0 (IBM Corp, Armonk, New York, NY). Any P values < 0.05 were considered statistically significant.
RESULTS
A total of 266 patients with CDI were enrolled during the two study periods. Available samples for this study were obtained from 147 patients. The median age was 78.5 years (IQR 19.5); 49% were male. The median CCI was 5 (IQR 2.75) and the median ATLAS score was 3 (IQR 3). The majority (69.6%) had a history of antibiotic consumption in the previous 3 months. In total, 52 out of 147 patients experienced an unfavorable outcome.
Comparison of the baseline characteristics at the onset of CDI between patients with and without unfavorable outcomes is given in Table 1. The two groups did not differ in any baseline characteristic, including age, sex, intake of antibiotics in the last 3 months, history of CDI, CCI, ATLAS score, and in most of the baseline laboratory findings, such as creatinine, low albumin levels, and white blood cells. Patients with unfavorable outcomes had significantly higher levels of PSP and CRP at the onset of CDI compared with those with favorable outcomes (Fig. 1).

To assess the prognostic accuracy of selected parameters for predicting unfavorable outcome in CDI, receiver operating characteristic curve analysis was performed for PSP, CRP, and ATLAS score (Supplementary Figure 1, https://links.lww.com/SHK/C667). PSP and CRP were selected as their levels differed significantly between patients with and without unfavorable outcomes. The ATLAS score was also included due to its common use in clinical practice to assess disease severity (20,21). PSP demonstrated the highest discriminative ability with an AUC of 0.648 [95% confidence interval (CI) 0.550–0.746, P: 0.003]. At the optimal cutoff value of 300 ng/mL, the risk of unfavorable outcome was significantly increased (OR 3.73, 95% CI 1.82–7.64, P <0.0001; Table 2). CRP had the slightly lowest prognostic value (AUC: 0.619, 95% CI 0.512–0.726, P: 0.030) with an optimal cutoff at 90 mg/L (OR 2.71, 95% CI 1.26–5.85, P: 0.012). The ATLAS score had limited predictive ability (AUC: 0.581, 95% CI 0.487–0.674, P: 0.090), and the OR for unfavorable outcome in patients with ATLAS score >3 was 1.065 (95% CI 0.54–2.01, P: 0.865).

To confirm the value of PSP in, we constructed a multivariate model. The model included PSP > 300 ng/mL and CRP > 90 mg/L. ATLAS score >3 was also included in the model. PSP > 300 ng/mL and CRP > 90 mg/L (P: 0.04), but not ATLAS score, were independently associated with unfavorable outcome (Supplementary Table 1, https://links.lww.com/SHK/C667).
DISCUSSION
In this prospective multicenter study, we managed to demonstrate for the first time that increased plasma PSP at the time of diagnosis can predict unfavorable outcome by CDI, that is, severe disease progression, relapse, organ dysfunction, and death. Notably, PSP outperformed conventional clinical and laboratory markers, including age, CCI, and ATLAS score, in predicting unfavorable outcome. Interestingly, patients with increases of both PSP and CRP experienced a much higher risk for unfavorable outcome.
To date, only a few biomarkers have been validated for early risk stratification in CDI. Commonly used markers, such as leukocytosis, elevated serum creatinine, low levels of albumin, and elevated CRP, have been used for the prediction of disease severity and risk of relapse. However, their role remains questionable, particularly for early risk assessment (4,6,7). The widely used ATLAS score lacks sufficient sensitivity and specificity for precise individual risk assessment in heterogeneous hospitalized populations (3,5,24), consistent with the findings of our study. Hence, there is an unmet need for reliable and accessible biomarkers capable of guiding early clinical decisions in CDI management. Although the discriminative performance of PSP in our cohort was moderate, increased PSP at the onset of CDI could alert clinicians to more aggressive and timely interventions, including advanced antimicrobial agents, closer monitoring, and early surgical consultation if needed (2,25).
PSP has recently emerged as a promising marker of infection, systemic inflammation, and sepsis, as it is rapidly upregulated in response to infectious or inflammatory stimuli, and studies show that its levels correlate with disease severity and outcomes in septic patients (9,12,26). PSP is believed to play a dual role in modulating immune responses and tissue repair processes, contributing to its sensitivity as an early indicator of clinical deterioration (8,11,27). Nevertheless, its potential application in gastrointestinal infections, such as CDI, remained unexplored before this study. PSP is secreted in response to cellular stress and inflammatory stimuli, particularly in the gastrointestinal tract where C. difficile exerts its pathogenic effects (28). In this context, increased PSP may represent a surrogate marker of the host’s early systemic response to toxin-mediated epithelial damage and immune activation, which, when intense, can lead to severe CDI and sepsis (29,30). Future mechanistic studies exploring PSP regulation in intestinal inflammation and epithelial barrier dysfunction could further elucidate its role in CDI.
Three main limitations of the present study need to be acknowledged: 1) the limited sample size, which warrants validation in larger and more diverse cohorts where the cost-effectiveness and clinical utility of PSP-guided management strategies can be elaborated; 2) the absence of nucleic acid amplification tests, which would also allow ribotyping and analysis of the association between O27 ribotypes and PSP; and 3) the absence of head-to-head comparisons with emerging biomarkers, such as fecal calprotectin (31,32).
In conclusion, this study provides novel evidence that plasma PSP is an independent prognostic biomarker for adverse clinical outcomes in CDI, especially when combined with CRP. To our knowledge, this is the first study evaluating PSP in the context of CDI, highlighting its potential to improve early risk stratification and guide therapeutic decision-making. Future multicentre studies are warranted to confirm these findings and to explore the optimal clinical use of PSP in the management of CDI.
